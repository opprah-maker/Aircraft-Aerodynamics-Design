Introduction

In the field of engineering, understanding the behavior of fluid flow around objects is fundamental for optimizing design and performance. This study investigates two essential cases in aerodynamics: the pressure distribution around a circular cylinder and the lift and drag characteristics of a NACA 0012 aerofoil. These analyses are critical for applications ranging from aircraft wings to automotive designs.

Objectives:

To analyze and compare theoretical and experimental surface pressure distributions on a circular cylinder.

To calculate and interpret lift and drag coefficients for a 3D NACA 0012 aerofoil, emphasizing its relevance in aerodynamic wing design.

A circular cylinder immersed in uniform flow experiences pressure differences due to fluid interaction. These pressure variations lead to a drag force, primarily caused by form drag, resulting from the pressure differential between the front and rear surfaces of the cylinder. Figure 1 illustrates the streamline patterns around a cylinder when subjected to a uniform flow. The higher pressure on the front half compared to the rear half results in a net drag force.

Figure 1: Streamlines around a circular cylinder in uniform flow.

Figure 1 demonstrates how the uniform flow interacts with a circular cylinder, creating distinct patterns of streamlines. The stagnation streamline splits the flow, with some streamlines moving over the top and others beneath the cylinder. At the stagnation point, located at the front of the cylinder, the fluid velocity drops to zero, resulting in maximum pressure. As the fluid continues around the cylinder, a pressure gradient forms, leading to a wake region at the rear where the flow separates from the surface. This wake region is characterized by low-pressure zones, which contribute significantly to the form drag acting on the cylinder. Understanding these flow patterns is essential for analysing pressure-induced drag and optimizing designs to minimize energy losses.

The pressure distribution on the cylinder surface can be visualized in Figure 2. Arrows indicate the pressure magnitude and direction at various points. As shown, the rear half of the cylinder experiences significantly lower pressure due to flow separation and the formation of a wake region, which is critical in understanding drag behaviour.
